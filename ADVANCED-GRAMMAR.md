## ğŸ² CONDITIONAL SENTENCES

### Overview
Conditional sentences express: "If X happens, then Y will happen"

### Type 0: General Truths / Scientific Facts
**Used for**: General truths, scientific facts, things that are always true

#### Structure
- **If + Present Simple, Present Simple**
- Can also use: When + Present Simple, Present Simple

#### Examples
- âœ… If you **heat** water to 100Â°C, it **boils**.
- âœ… If you **mix** red and blue, you **get** purple.
- âœ… When the sun **sets**, it **gets** dark.
- âœ… If you **freeze** water, it **turns** to ice.

**Note**: "If" and "When" can be interchangeable in Type 0

---

### Type 1: Real Possibilities in Future
**Used for**: Likely or possible situations in the future

#### Structure
- **If + Present Simple, will + Base Verb**
- Also can use: may, might, could, should, must instead of will

#### Examples
- âœ… If it **rains**, I **will stay** at home.
- âœ… If you **study** hard, you **will pass** the exam.
- âœ… If I **have** time, I **will help** you.
- âœ… If she **calls**, I **will tell** her.

#### With Other Modals
- âœ… If it rains, I **might stay** at home. (possibility)
- âœ… If it rains, I **could stay** at home. (ability/possibility)
- âœ… If it rains, I **should stay** at home. (advice)
- âœ… If it rains, I **must stay** at home. (necessity)

#### First Conditional Variations

**Imperative in Result Clause**:
- âœ… If you see him, **tell him to call me**.
- âœ… If you need help, **ask me**.

**Present Continuous in if-clause**:
- âœ… If you are **looking** for a job, **I will help** you.
- âœ… If it is **raining** tomorrow, **we will cancel** the trip.

**Going to in Result Clause**:
- âœ… If you finish early, **we are going to celebrate**.

---

### Type 2: Unreal / Hypothetical Present
**Used for**: Imaginary or unlikely situations in present or future

#### Structure
- **If + Past Simple, would + Base Verb**
- **Important**: Use "were" (not was) for all subjects in if-clause

#### Examples
- âœ… If I **had** a million dollars, I **would buy** a house.
- âœ… If I **were** you, I **would accept** the offer.
- âœ… If she **knew** the answer, she **would tell** us.
- âœ… If we **had** more time, **we would visit** more places.

#### Second Conditional Variations

**Past Continuous in if-clause**:
- âœ… If I **were working** now, I **would be busy**.
- âœ… If it **were raining** right now, **we would stay** inside.

**Could in Result Clause** (ability/possibility):
- âœ… If I had a car, I **could drive** you there.
- âœ… If I had more money, I **could travel** more.

**Might in Result Clause** (less certain possibility):
- âœ… If I had time, I **might go** to the party.

**With Continuous Forms**:
- âœ… If I **had** more money, I **would be traveling** around the world.
- âœ… If I **were living** in New York, **I would visit** Central Park every day.

### Type 3: Unreal Past
**Used for**: Hypothetical situations in the past (didn't happen)

#### Structure
- **If + Past Perfect, would have + Past Participle**

#### Examples
- âœ… If I **had known** about the meeting, I **would have attended**.
- âœ… If she **had studied** harder, she **would have passed** the exam.
- âœ… If we **had left** earlier, **we would have arrived** on time.
- âœ… If he **had called** me, I **would have helped** him.

#### Third Conditional Variations

**Past Perfect Continuous in if-clause**:
- âœ… If I **had been studying** all day, I **would have been tired**.
- âœ… If she **had been working** there, she **would have known** about the change.

**Could Have / Might Have in Result Clause**:
- âœ… If I had known, I **could have helped**. (ability/possibility)
- âœ… If he had arrived earlier, he **might have seen** her. (possibility)

**With Progressive Forms**:
- âœ… If I **had known**, I **would have been waiting** for you.
- âœ… If they **had informed** us, **we would have been preparing**.

### Mixed Conditionals

#### Mixed Type 2 & 3 (Past Condition, Present Result)
- **If + Past Perfect, would + Base Verb** (Present result)
- Result refers to present time

**Examples**:
- âœ… If I **had studied** medicine, I **would be** a doctor now. (past condition, present result)
- âœ… If she **hadn't missed** the bus, **she would be** here now.
- âœ… If we **had bought** tickets, **we would be watching** the movie now.

#### Mixed Type 3 & 2 (Present Condition, Past Result)
- **If + Past Simple, would have + Past Participle** (Past result)
- Result refers to past time

**Examples**:
- âœ… If I **spoke** French, I **would have gotten** the job. (present condition, past result)
- âœ… If she **were** more careful, **she wouldn't have broken** the vase.
- âœ… If we **had** a car, **we would have driven** there yesterday.

### Special Conditional Structures

#### Unless (If...not)
- "Unless" = "If not"
- Follows the same structure as First Conditional

**Examples**:
- âœ… Unless you **study** hard, you **won't pass**. (= If you don't study hard, you won't pass)
- âœ… I won't go **unless** you **come** with me. (= I won't go if you don't come with me)
- âœ… Unless it **rains**, we **will have** a picnic.

#### I wish / If only
- **I wish / If only + Past Simple** (Present desire)
- **I wish / If only + Past Perfect** (Regret about past)

**Present Desires**:
- âœ… I wish I **had** a car. (but I don't)
- âœ… If only I **were** rich. (but I'm not)
- âœ… She wishes she **spoke** French. (but she doesn't)

**Past Regrets**:
- âœ… I wish I **had studied** harder. (but I didn't)
- âœ… If only I **had known** about it. (but I didn't)
- âœ… He wishes he **hadn't made** that mistake. (but he did)

#### Inversion in Conditionals
- More formal, literary
- Omit "if", invert subject and auxiliary

**Type 1 (Present)**:
- âœ… **Should** you need help, **call me**. (= If you should need help...)
- âœ… **Were** I you, **I would accept**. (= If I were you...)
- âœ… **Had** I known, **I would have helped**. (= If I had known...)

**Type 2 (Past)**:
- âœ… **Were** he here, **he would help**. (= If he were here...)
- âœ… **Had** I seen him, **I would have said** hello. (= If I had seen him...)

### Wish + Infinitive / Gerund

#### Wish + Object + Infinitive (Present desire for someone else)
- âœ… I wish him **to leave**. (= I want him to leave)
- âœ… She wishes her son **to study** harder.
- âœ… They wish us **to succeed**.

#### Wish + Gerund (Want to stop something)
- âœ… I wish he would **stop** talking. (= I want him to stop)
- âœ… She wishes it would **stop** raining.
- âœ… They wish the problem would **go away**.

---

### Hope vs Wish

#### Hope (Possible)
- Expectation that something is possible
- âœ… I hope **it rains** tomorrow. (possible)
- âœ… She hopes **to get** the job. (possible)
- âœ… They hope **that we can come**.

#### Wish (Unreal/Impossible)
- Desire for something unlikely or impossible
- âœ… I wish **it would rain**. (not raining)
- âœ… She wishes **she could fly**. (impossible)
- âœ… They wish **they had more money**. (don't have)

## ğŸ”‡ PASSIVE VOICE

### Overview
- **Active Voice**: Subject does the action (e.g., John wrote the report)
- **Passive Voice**: Subject receives the action (e.g., The report was written by John)
- **Passive Formula**: be + past participle (V3)

### When to Use Passive Voice

1. **Unknown Agent** (Pelaku tidak diketahui)
   - âœ… My wallet **was stolen**. (by whom? unknown)

2. **Obvious Agent** (Pelaku sudah jelas)
   - âœ… The thief **was arrested**. (by police - obvious)

3. **Focus on Result** (Fokus pada hasil, bukan pelaku)
   - âœ… The project **has been completed**. (focus on completion)

4. **Object More Important than Subject**
   - âœ… The Mona Lisa **was painted** by Da Vinci. (Mona Lisa = focus)

5. **Scientific/Formal Writing**
   - âœ… The experiment **was conducted** to test...

---

### Passive Voice in All Tenses

#### Present Simple
**Active**: Subject + V1/V1+s/es + Object
**Passive**: Subject + am/is/are + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: She **writes** reports.
- âœ… Passive: Reports **are written** by her.
- âœ… Active: He **fixes** computers.
- âœ… Passive: Computers **are fixed** by him.
- âœ… Active: They **make** products.
- âœ… Passive: Products **are made** by them.

#### Present Continuous
**Active**: Subject + am/is/are + V-ing + Object
**Passive**: Subject + am/is/are + being + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **am writing** a report.
- âœ… Passive: A report **is being written** by me.
- âœ… Active: She **is reading** the email.
- âœ… Passive: The email **is being read** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **are discussing** the project.
- âœ… Passive: The project **is being discussed** by them.

#### Present Perfect
**Active**: Subject + have/has + V3 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + have/has + been + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **have finished** the task.
- âœ… Passive: The task **has been finished**.
- âœ… Active: She **has submitted** the report.
- âœ… Passive: The report **has been submitted** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **have completed** the project.
- âœ… Passive: The project **has been completed** by them.

#### Past Simple
**Active**: Subject + V2 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + was/were + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **wrote** the email.
- âœ… Passive: The email **was written** by me.
- âœ… Active: She **bought** a car.
- âœ… Passive: A car **was bought** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **finished** the work.
- âœ… Passive: The work **was finished** by them.

#### Past Continuous
**Active**: Subject + was/were + V-ing + Object
**Passive**: Subject + was/were + being + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **was writing** the report.
- âœ… Passive: The report **was being written** by me.
- âœ… Active: She **was making** dinner.
- âœ… Passive: Dinner **was being made** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **were discussing** the issue.
- âœ… Passive: The issue **was being discussed** by them.

#### Past Perfect
**Active**: Subject + had + V3 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + had + been + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **had finished** before he arrived.
- âœ… Passive: The work **had been finished** before he arrived.
- âœ… Active: She **had already left**.
- âœ… Passive: She **had already left**. (intransitive - no passive)
- âœ… Active: They **had completed** the project.
- âœ… Passive: The project **had been completed** by them.

#### Future Simple
**Active**: Subject + will + V1 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + will be + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **will finish** the report.
- âœ… Passive: The report **will be finished** by me.
- âœ… Active: She **will write** the letter.
- âœ… Passive: The letter **will be written** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **will complete** the task.
- âœ… Passive: The task **will be completed** by them.

#### Future Continuous
**Active**: Subject + will be + V-ing + Object
**Passive**: Subject + will be being + V3 (+ by Agent)
*Rare in passive, usually avoided*

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **will be writing** the report tomorrow.
- âœ… Passive: The report **will be being written** by me tomorrow. (awkward)

#### Future Perfect
**Active**: Subject + will have + V3 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + will have been + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: I **will have finished** by next week.
- âœ… Passive: The report **will have been finished** by next week.
- âœ… Active: She **will have completed** the project.
- âœ… Passive: The project **will have been completed** by her.

#### Modals in Passive
**Active**: Subject + Modal + V1 + Object
**Passive**: Subject + Modal + be + V3 (+ by Agent)

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: You **must finish** this.
- âœ… Passive: This **must be finished**.
- âœ… Active: She **can write** the report.
- âœ… Passive: The report **can be written** by her.
- âœ… Active: They **should complete** the task.

- âœ… The report **can** be written.

- âœ… The report **should** be written.
- âœ… The report **would** be written.
- âœ… The report **might** be written.
- âœ… The report **must** be written.
- âœ… The report **may** be written.

### Getting Passive from Active (Step-by-Step)

**Example 1**: Active to Passive
1. Active: "The manager **wrote** the report."
2. Identify: Subject = manager, Verb = wrote, Object = report
3. Move Object to Subject position: "The report..."
4. Change verb to passive: "was written"
5. Add "by" + original Subject: "by the manager"
6. Result: "The report **was written** by the manager."

**Example 2**: Active to Passive
1. Active: "She **is writing** a letter."
2. Identify: Subject = she, Verb = is writing, Object = a letter
3. Move Object to Subject: "A letter..."
4. Change verb to passive: "is being written"
5. Add "by" + original Subject: "by her"
6. Result: "A letter **is being written** by her."

### Verbs with Two Objects (Passive Possibilities)

Some verbs have two objects: Indirect Object (person) + Direct Object (thing)

**Verbs with Two Objects**:
- give, show, send, tell, lend, hand, offer, pass, sell, write, read

**Example**: "She gave me a book."
1. Active: She gave me a book. (me = indirect, a book = direct)
2. Passive 1 (Focus on person): I **was given** a book by her.
3. Passive 2 (Focus on thing): A book **was given to me** by her.

**More Examples**:
- Active: "He sent me a letter."
  - Passive 1: I **was sent** a letter by him.
  - Passive 2: A letter **was sent to me** by him.

- Active: "They told us the news."
  - Passive 1: We **were told** the news by them.
  - Passive 2: The news **was told to us** by them.

**Examples**:
- âœ… Active: The car **belongs** to me.
- âŒ Passive: The car is belonged to me. (WRONG)

- âœ… Active: It **seems** expensive.
- âŒ Passive: It is seemed expensive. (WRONG)

### Passive Voice with "Get" (Informal)

In informal speech, sometimes "get" is used instead of "be" in passive.

**Examples**:

- âœ… She **got stuck** in traffic.

- Direct: "The project **will be completed**."
- Reported: She said that the project **would be completed**.

## ğŸ’¬ REPORTED SPEECH (Indirect Speech)

### Overview
- **Direct Speech**: Exact words someone said
  - âœ… She said, "I am working today."
- **Reported Speech**: Reporting what someone said (indirect)
  - âœ… She said that she **was working** that day.

### Tense Changes (Backshifting)

When reporting speech in the past (e.g., "He said..."), tenses usually shift back one step:

| Direct Speech | Reported Speech |
|---------------|----------------|
| Present Simple | Past Simple |
| Present Continuous | Past Continuous |
| Present Perfect | Past Perfect |
| Past Simple | Past Perfect |
| Past Continuous | Past Perfect Continuous |
| Will | Would |
| Can | Could |
| May | Might |
| Must | Had to |

---

### Reporting Verbs

#### Verbs for Statements
- say, tell, explain, mention, state, announce, declare, claim, add, reply, answer

**Examples**:
- âœ… He **said** that he was busy.
- âœ… She **told** me that she would come.
- âœ… They **explained** that the problem was solved.

**Say vs Tell**:
- âœ… He **said** (to me) that...
- âœ… He **told** me that...
- âŒ He said me that... (WRONG - say doesn't take object)
- âŒ He told that... (WRONG - tell needs object)

#### Verbs for Questions
- ask, want to know, wonder, inquire

**Examples**:
- âœ… He **asked** me if I was coming.
- âœ… She **wanted to know** where I lived.
- âœ… They **wondered** why he was late.

**Direct to Reported Questions**:
- Direct: "Are you coming?" (Yes/No question)
- Reported: He **asked me if** I was coming.

- Direct: "Where do you live?" (Wh-question)
- Reported: He **asked me where** I lived.

- Direct: "What are you doing?" (Wh-question)
- Reported: He **asked me what** I was doing.

**Note**: Reported questions do NOT use question word order (no inversion)

#### Verbs for Commands/Requests
- tell, order, command, ask, beg, advise, warn, request

**Examples**:
- Direct: "Come here!"
- Reported: He **told me to come** here.

- Direct: "Don't do that!"
- Reported: He **told me not to do** that.

- Direct: "Please help me."
- Reported: He **asked me to help** him.

- Direct: "Wait here."
- Reported: She **ordered me to wait** there.

**Formula**: reporting verb + object + to-infinitive

### Pronoun Changes

Pronouns change to match the perspective of the reporter.

**Examples**:
- Direct: "I am happy." (John said)
- Reported: John said that **he** was happy.

- Direct: "You are right." (She told me)
- Reported: She told me that **I** was right.

- Direct: "She is my friend." (He said)
- Reported: He said that **she was his** friend.

- Direct: "We will go tomorrow." (They said)
- Reported: They said that **they would go the next day**.

**General Rule**:
- I â†’ he/she (depending on speaker)
- You â†’ I/they (depending on who was addressed)
- We â†’ they
- My â†’ his/her
- Your â†’ my/their

- âœ… I saw him **cross** the street. (watched the complete action)
- âœ… She heard the door **open**.
- âœ… I felt someone **touch** my shoulder.
- âœ… We watched the sun **rise**.

**Gerund vs Infinitive with Perception Verbs**:
- âœ… I saw him **cross** the street. (complete action)
- âœ… I saw him **crossing** the street. (action in progress)

---

### Verbs + Both (Gerund OR Infinitive) with Different Meanings

#### 1. Stop
- **Stop + Gerund**: Quit a habit
  - âœ… I stopped **smoking**. (I quit smoking)
- **Stop + Infinitive**: Pause to do something
  - âœ… I stopped **to smoke**. (I paused what I was doing to smoke)

#### 2. Remember
- **Remember + Gerund**: Recall doing something in the past
  - âœ… I remember **locking** the door. (I recall that I locked it)
- **Remember + Infinitive**: Don't forget to do something in the future
  - âœ… Remember **to lock** the door. (Don't forget to lock it later)

#### 3. Forget
- **Forget + Gerund**: Don't recall doing something
  - âœ… I forgot **locking** the door. (I don't remember that I locked it)
- **Forget + Infinitive**: Forget to do something
  - âœ… I forgot **to lock** the door. (I didn't lock it because I forgot)

#### 4. Try
- **Try + Gerund**: Attempt something, see if it works
  - âœ… I tried **calling** him. (I attempted to call him)
- **Try + Infinitive**: Make an effort
  - âœ… I tried **to call** him. (I made an effort to call him)
- *Note: Often similar in meaning, slight difference*

#### 5. Regret
- **Regret + Gerund**: Regret something done in the past
  - âœ… I regret **telling** him the truth. (I wish I hadn't told him)
- **Regret + Infinitive**: Regret having to say something (formal)
  - âœ… I regret **to inform** you that... (I'm sorry to say...)

#### 6. Mean
- **Mean + Gerund**: Involve
  - âœ… This job **means working** long hours. (Working long hours is part of the job)
- **Mean + Infinitive**: Intend
  - âœ… I meant **to call** you. (I intended to call you)

#### 7. Go on
- **Go on + Gerund**: Continue with the same activity
  - âœ… She went on **talking** for hours. (She continued talking)
- **Go on + Infinitive**: Change to a new activity
  - âœ… She went on **to explain** her theory. (She started explaining after something else)

#### 8. Start / Begin
- Can use both (no difference in meaning)
  - âœ… I started **working**.
  - âœ… I started **to work**.
  - *Both correct, same meaning*

#### 9. Like / Love / Hate
- Can use both (no difference in meaning)
  - âœ… I like **working** here.
  - âœ… I like **to work** here.
  - *Both correct, same meaning*

## ğŸ”„ PARTICIPLES

### Overview
- **Present Participle**: verb + -ing
- **Past Participle**: verb + -ed (regular) / V3 (irregular)

### Present Participles

#### Uses

**1. Continuous Tenses**
- âœ… I **am working**.
- âœ… She **was reading**.

**2. Adjectives**
- âœ… An **interesting** book.
- âœ… A **boring** movie.

**3. Reduced Relative Clauses**
- âœ… The man **standing by the door** is John. (= The man who is standing by the door)
- âœ… The girl **wearing the red dress** is my sister. (= The girl who is wearing...)

**4. Adverbial Clauses (Simultaneous Actions)**
- âœ… **Walking down the street**, I saw my friend. (= While I was walking down the street, I saw my friend)
- âœ… **Feeling tired**, I went to bed early.

**5. Cause/Result**
- âœ… **Not knowing** the answer, I remained silent. (= Because I didn't know the answer...)
- âœ… **Being tired**, she couldn't concentrate. (= Because she was tired...)

---

### Past Participles

#### Uses

**1. Perfect Tenses**
- âœ… I **have finished**.
- âœ… She **had left**.

**2. Passive Voice**
- âœ… The report **was written**.
- âœ… The work **has been completed**.

**3. Adjectives**
- âœ… A **broken** window.
- âœ… **Fried** chicken.

**4. Reduced Relative Clauses (Passive)**
- âœ… The book **written by Smith** is popular. (= The book which was written by Smith)
- âœ… The **stolen** car was found. (= The car that was stolen)

---

### Perfect Participles

#### Structure
- **Active**: Having + V3
- **Passive**: Having been + V3

#### Uses

**1. Action Completed Before Another Action**
- âœ… **Having finished** my work, I went home. (= After I had finished my work...)
- âœ… **Having eaten** breakfast, she went to work.

**2. Perfect Passive**
- âœ… **Having been invited** to the party, I bought a gift. (= After I had been invited...)

---

### Dangling Participles (Common Mistake)

A dangling participle occurs when the subject of the participle clause doesn't match the subject of the main clause.

**Incorrect Examples**:
- âŒ **Walking down the street**, the dog bit me. (The dog wasn't walking)
- âŒ **Reading the book**, the phone rang. (The phone wasn't reading)
- âŒ **Driving too fast**, the accident happened. (The accident wasn't driving)

**Correct Versions**:
- âœ… **Walking down the street**, I was bitten by the dog.
- âœ… **While walking down the street**, I saw a dog that bit me.
- âœ… **Driving too fast**, he caused the accident.
- âœ… **While reading the book**, I heard the phone ring.

**Rule**: The subject of the participle phrase MUST be the same as the subject of the main clause.

## ğŸ”— RELATIVE CLAUSES

### Overview
Relative clauses describe or identify a noun. Also called "adjective clauses."

### Relative Pronouns

| Relative Pronoun | Used For | Example |
|------------------|----------|---------|
| **who** | People (subject) | The man **who** called me |
| **whom** | People (object) | The man **whom** I saw |
| **whose** | Possession | The girl **whose** name is Mary |
| **which** | Things/animals | The book **which** I read |
| **that** | People/things (restrictive only) | The car **that** I bought |
| **where** | Places | The office **where** I work |
| **when** | Times | The day **when** I arrived |
| **why** | Reasons | The reason **why** I came |

---

### Types of Relative Clauses

#### 1. Defining Relative Clauses (Essential Information)
- Identifies which person/thing is being talked about
- NO commas
- Use "that" or "who/which"

**Examples**:
- âœ… The man **who called me** is my friend. (identifies which man)
- âœ… The book **that I read** is interesting. (identifies which book)
- âœ… The office **where I work** is in Jakarta. (identifies which office)

#### 2. Non-Defining Relative Clauses (Extra Information)
- Adds extra information about a noun already identified
- WITH commas
- CANNOT use "that"
- MUST use "who/which"

**Examples**:
- âœ… My brother, **who lives in New York**, is coming to visit. (extra info - brother already identified)
- âœ… This book, **which I bought yesterday**, is great. (extra info - book already identified)
- âœ… Jakarta, **where I was born**, is a big city. (extra info - Jakarta already known)

---

### Omitting Relative Pronouns

**Rule**: Can omit relative pronoun when it is the OBJECT of the relative clause (NOT the subject).

**Can Omit (Object Position)**:
- âœ… The man **(whom)** I saw is my friend. (= The man I saw...)
- âœ… The book **(which)** I read is great. (= The book I read...)
- âœ… The house **(that)** she bought is beautiful. (= The house she bought...)

**CANNOT Omit (Subject Position)**:
- âŒ The man **who** called me is my friend. (who = subject of relative clause, cannot omit)
- âŒ The book **which** is on the table is mine. (which = subject of relative clause, cannot omit)

---

### "Whose" (Possessive Relative Pronoun)
- Shows possession in relative clauses

**Examples**:
- âœ… The girl **whose name is Mary** is my friend.
- âœ… The man **whose car was stolen** is at the police station.
- âœ… The company **whose products we use** is closing down.

---

### "Where" (Relative Adverb)
- Used for places

**Examples**:
- âœ… The office **where I work** is modern.
- âœ… The city **where I was born** is beautiful.
- âœ… The restaurant **where we ate** was expensive.

---

### "When" (Relative Adverb)
- Used for times

**Examples**:
- âœ… The day **when I arrived** was sunny.
- âœ… The year **when I graduated** was 2020.
- âœ… The time **when we met** was unforgettable.

---

### "Why" (Relative Adverb)
- Used for reasons (only with "the reason")

**Example**:
- âœ… The reason **why I came** is to help you.

---

### Reduced Relative Clauses

Can omit relative pronoun + be verb in certain cases.

#### 1. Active Relative Clauses (Present Participle)
- âœ… The man **who is standing** by the door â†’ The man **standing** by the door.
- âœ… The girl **who is wearing** red â†’ The girl **wearing** red.

#### 2. Passive Relative Clauses (Past Participle)
- âœ… The book **which was written** by Smith â†’ The book **written** by Smith.
- âœ… The car **which was stolen** â†’ The **stolen** car.

---

### Preposition + Which/Whom

In formal English, preposition comes before relative pronoun.

**Formal**:
- âœ… The house **in which** I live is old.
- âœ… The person **to whom** I spoke was helpful.
- âœ… The book **from which** I learned is famous.

**Informal**:
- âœ… The house **which I live in** is old.
- âœ… The person **who I spoke to** was helpful.
- âœ… The book **which I learned from** is famous.

**Note**: If using "that", preposition CANNOT come before it. Must come after.
